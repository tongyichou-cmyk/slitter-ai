# Slitting Line Changeover Guide: Reduce Setup Time from 90 to 25 Minutes

A slitting line changeover is a direct cost. Every minute the line is stopped is production capacity that can never be recovered. On a line running 300 m/min with 3 changeovers per shift, each minute saved across those 3 changeovers adds up to real throughput.

This guide applies SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die) principles to slitting line changeover, with concrete actions for each phase.

---

## Understanding Where Time Goes

Before improving, measure. A typical 90-minute changeover breaks down roughly like this:

| Phase | Typical time | Reducible to |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Waiting for tools / paperwork | 15 min | 0 min (pre-stage) |
| Deceleration, threading out old strip | 5 min | 4 min |
| Unlocking and removing arbor tooling | 20 min | 5 min (hydraulic nut) |
| Cleaning arbor, measuring | 8 min | 6 min |
| Building new knife stack | 20 min | 8 min (offline pre-build) |
| Installing and locking knife stack | 12 min | 4 min (hydraulic nut) |
| Rethreading, tension setup | 8 min | 5 min |
| First-piece inspection and adjustment | 12 min | 8 min |
| **Total** | **100 min** | **~40 min internal + 0 wait = ~40 min** |

The biggest gains come from converting **internal** activities (done while line is stopped) to **external** activities (done while line is still running).

---

## Phase 0: Pre-Stage While the Line Is Still Running

This is the highest-leverage phase. Everything here happens while the current coil is finishing.

### What to do while the line runs:
1. **Pull the next job's setup sheet** — confirm material grade, thickness, finished widths, number of strips, coil OD/ID
2. **Pull the knife set** — locate the correct knife diameter, thickness, and quantity from the toolroom
3. **Pull the spacer set** — calculate the spacer stack for the new width configuration; physically gather and verify
4. **Pre-assemble the knife stack offline** — using a dedicated assembly mandrel, build the complete knife-and-spacer stack for both upper and lower arbors
5. **Check knife condition** — verify knife sharpness, edge integrity, and runout on the offline mandrel before bringing to the machine
6. **Stage hydraulic pump** — position the hydraulic pump next to the machine; verify oil level and connector condition
7. **Confirm clearance target** — calculate and document the physical clearance per side for the new material
8. **Prepare the new coil** — have it at the uncoiler, ID ready for the mandrel

**Target: everything in this list completed before the line stops.**

## Phase 1: Line Stop and Arbor Teardown (Target: 9 min)

### Step 1: Decelerate and thread out (3 min)
- Decelerate the line on the tail end of the current coil
- Thread the strip tail completely out through the slitter and recoiler
- Clear loose scrap and trimmings from the cut zone

### Step 2: Release hydraulic nut (1 min)
- Connect the hydraulic pump to the hydraulic nut
- Open the bleed valve — pressure drops to zero
- Disconnect the pump; unscrew the hydraulic nut by hand (no tools needed)

### Step 3: Slide off tooling (5 min)
- Slide the complete knife-and-spacer stack off both arbors as a unit
- Set aside for post-run inspection
- Wipe the arbor surface and taper seat with a clean cloth

---

## Phase 2: Arbor Inspection and Preparation (Target: 5 min)

Do not skip this phase — debris or scoring on the arbor will cause repeatability problems in the new setup.

### Check arbor surface (2 min)
- Visual inspection for nicks, burrs, or scoring marks on the arbor OD
- Use a fine file or emery paper to remove raised burrs if present
- Wipe with anti-corrosion oil

### Check arbor runout (2 min)
- Place a dial indicator on the arbor OD near the tooling zone
- Rotate the arbor by hand; TIR should be ≤ 0.01mm
- If TIR exceeds 0.02mm, investigate before proceeding (bent arbor or worn bearing)

### Measure arbor length (1 min)
- Verify the arbor tooling zone length matches the expected total stack width
- This catches any spacer arithmetic errors before installation

---

## Phase 3: Install Pre-Built Knife Stack (Target: 7 min)

Because the knife stack was pre-built offline, installation is sliding and locking only.

### Step 1: Slide the pre-built stack onto the arbor (2 min)
- Confirm orientation — upper vs. lower knives, correct interleave sequence
- Slide the full stack onto the arbor in one motion

### Step 2: Verify stack alignment (2 min)
- Use a dial indicator to confirm knife runout (TIR ≤ 0.010mm per knife position)
- Check lateral knife position against the target spacing (use a width gage or set block)

### Step 3: Install and pressurize the hydraulic nut (1 min)
- Thread the hydraulic nut on by hand — no torque tool needed
- Connect the hydraulic pump
- Slowly pressurize to the manufacturer-specified setpoint
- Close the shutoff valve; disconnect the pump

### Step 4: Confirm seating (2 min)
- Verify the pressure gauge holds steady (no pressure drop within 1 min = no leak)
- Check that all knife faces are seated flush against the spacers

---

## Phase 4: Set Clearance and Verify (Target: 5 min)

### Step 1: Set arbor horizontal clearance (2 min)
- For mechanical clearance adjustment: use the clearance shim from the setup sheet
- For CNC arbor positioning: enter the target clearance per side; jog to position
- Verify with a feeler gauge at the engagement zone

### Step 2: Set penetration depth (1 min)
- Lower the upper arbor to the target penetration depth
- Verify with the penetration gauge or dial indicator reference

### Step 3: Verify with a feeler gauge pass (2 min)
- Check clearance at 3 knife positions across the arbor width
- Variation across positions should be ≤ 0.005mm

---

## Phase 5: Thread New Strip and First-Piece Inspection (Target: 9 min)

### Step 1: Thread the new coil (4 min)
- Mount the new coil on the uncoiler
- Thread the strip through the entry guide, slitter, separator, and recoiler
- Set tension per the job setup sheet

### Step 2: Slow-speed trial cut (2 min)
- Run 2–3 meters at 10–15% of normal line speed
- Stop the line

### Step 3: Edge quality inspection (3 min)
- Inspect burr height on slit edges (target ≤ 5% of thickness for steel)
- Inspect rollover depth
- Measure slit width on 2–3 strips with a calibrated tape or width gauge
- If any strip is out of tolerance, adjust clearance or spacer and re-test

---

## Phase 6: Run-Up to Full Speed (Target: 2 min)

- Accelerate in controlled steps: 25% → 50% → 75% → 100%
- Monitor tension at each step; adjust if strip wanders or sags
- Confirm at full speed: no vibration, no edge tracking issues, no strip wander

**Total elapsed time: ~37 minutes internal + 0 wait = under 40 minutes.**  
With team practice and pre-staged tooling, 25 minutes is achievable.

## The Offline Tooling Assembly Station

The single biggest enabler of fast changeover is an **offline tooling assembly mandrel** — a spare shaft that matches the arbor diameter, mounted on a bench stand. This lets one person build the entire knife-and-spacer stack while the line is running, while another operator handles the current coil.

### What the station needs:
- A hardened shaft matching arbor diameter (tolerance ± 0.002mm)
- Bench stand with rotation capability
- Dial indicator arm for runout verification
- Set of all spacer sizes for typical job mix
- Dedicated storage for assembled knife sets (labeled by job/material)

Cost: typically $300–800 to build in-house. Payback: recoverable on the first week of changeovers.

## Summary: SMED Levers Ranked by Impact

| Lever | Time saved | Investment |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Pre-stage tooling offline | 15–20 min | $0 (process only) |
| Offline assembly mandrel | 15 min | Low ($500–$1,000) |
| Hydraulic nut (vs manual) | 15–20 min | Medium ($2,000–$5,000) |
| Pre-calculated setup sheets | 5–10 min | $0 (process only) |
| Dedicated tooling carts | 5 min | Low ($200–$500) |

The biggest gains are free — they require only process discipline, not capital.
