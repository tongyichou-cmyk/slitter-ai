# Slitting Machine: Technical Overview

A slitting machine is an industrial processing line that unwinds a wide master coil, cuts it into multiple narrower strips using circular rotating knives, and rewinds each strip into individual coils. It is a core capital asset in metal service centers, steel mills, and precision strip manufacturing operations worldwide.

---

## Operating Principle

The slitting machine does not saw or grind material — it shears it. A pair of upper and lower circular knives mounted on parallel arbors overlap slightly in the vertical plane and are separated by a precise horizontal clearance. As the coil passes through the slitter head at line speed, the material fractures cleanly between each knife pair.

A single slitting line pass can produce 2 to 40+ strips simultaneously, depending on the arbor length, minimum strip width, and material type. The cut width accuracy of a well-tooled precision line is ±0.1 mm or better.

---

## Machine Components

### Uncoiler (Decoiler)

The uncoiler carries the incoming master coil on an expandable mandrel. Single-head uncoilers hold one coil; double-head units allow the next coil to be pre-loaded for faster changeover. Coil weights range from 5 tonnes on light-gauge lines to 50 tonnes on heavy-gauge equipment.

### Straightener / Leveler

Corrects the coil set (camber, crossbow, and longitudinal bow) imparted during coiling. A 5-roll or 7-roll straightener is standard for light-gauge lines; heavier material requires cassette-style roller levelers with larger roll diameters. Inadequate straightening causes strip camber downstream and width variation at the slitter head.

### Feed Table and Loop Control

A motorized feed table advances the strip to the slitter head. A loop pit or accumulator before the slitter provides a buffer that decouples the uncoiler speed from the slitter head speed, enabling continuous feed without speed mismatches.

### Slitter Head

The core of the machine. The slitter head carries the upper and lower arbors, each loaded with alternating knives and spacers. The arbor length determines the maximum coil width processable. Arbors rotate at the same peripheral speed as the strip feed rate; the slitter head typically floats vertically to maintain consistent knife engagement depth.

**Arbor specifications by machine class:**

| Class | Arbor Diameter | Material Thickness | Max Coil Width |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Light-gauge | 75–100 mm | 0.1–3 mm | 1,250–2,000 mm |
| Medium-gauge | 100–150 mm | 1–8 mm | 1,500–2,500 mm |
| Heavy-gauge | 150–200 mm | 3–25 mm | 1,000–2,000 mm |

### Separator / Scrap Winder

After the slitter head, edge trim (scrap) must be removed from the slit strips. A scrap winder or chopper handles the edge trim continuously. An undersized scrap system is a common production bottleneck — the scrap winder must match the maximum line speed at full trim width.

### Tension Stand and Recoiler

The recoiler winds each slit strip onto individual mandrel shafts or coil separators. Consistent strip tension during recoiling determines coil shape quality. Individual strip tension control via dancer rolls or torque-limited shafts is required for high-value materials. The recoiler mandrel expands and contracts hydraulically to accept and release finished coils.

## Machine Types

### Standard Rotary Slitting Line

The universal configuration for metal coil processing. Handles the full range of metals from soft aluminum to stainless steel in the 0.1–12 mm thickness range. Available in light, medium, and heavy-gauge variants.

### High-Speed Light-Gauge Line

Designed for speeds above 300 m/min on thin material (0.1–1.5 mm). Precision-ground arbors, automated tension control, and threading assist systems are standard. Loop control and strip handling require more engineering at high speed to prevent strip flutter and edge damage.

### Heavy-Gauge and Plate Slitting Line

For material thicknesses of 3–25 mm. Line speeds are lower (20–80 m/min) but cutting forces are significantly higher. Heavy arbor construction, high-torque drive systems, and reinforced slitter head frames are required. Knife arbor diameter is 150–200 mm to resist deflection under cutting loads.

### Combination Slitting / Cut-to-Length Line

Integrates a flying shear or rotary knife downstream of the slitter head, allowing production of both slit coil and flat sheet from the same line. Capital cost is 30–50% higher than a dedicated slitting line, but justified when production mix requires both output formats.

### Foil Slitting Line

Specialized equipment for aluminum foil, copper foil, and battery electrode materials below 0.1 mm thickness. Requires air-bearing or hydrodynamic arbors, micro-clearance knife setup systems, and cleanroom-compatible enclosures for battery applications. Strip handling is entirely different from metal slitting — tension levels are measured in Newtons per meter width.

## Key Performance Parameters

### Line Speed

Operating speed directly determines throughput. Practical line speed is limited by the weakest element in the system — often the scrap winder, recoiler tension control, or strip handling between slitter head and recoiler.

| Material | Typical Line Speed |
|----------|--------------------|
| Mild carbon steel 0.5–2 mm | 200–500 m/min |
| Stainless steel 1–3 mm | 80–200 m/min |
| Aluminum alloy 0.5–2 mm | 150–400 m/min |
| Heavy gauge steel 5–15 mm | 20–60 m/min |
| Copper foil 0.01–0.1 mm | 50–150 m/min |

### Strip Width Accuracy

Tolerance on slit strip width depends on spacer stack accuracy and arbor runout. Standard lines: ±0.2–0.5 mm. Precision lines with servo-positioned arbors and ±0.002 mm spacers: ±0.05–0.1 mm.

### Coil Weight Capacity

Determines the maximum master coil size processable. A mismatch between available coil supplier weight and machine capacity forces partial coil processing and increases changeover frequency.

## Tooling: The Multiplier on Machine Performance

The slitting machine determines what is possible. The tooling determines what is achieved in practice.

**Knife arbor stack** — alternating knives and spacers determine the width of each slit strip. Spacer tolerance directly controls width accuracy. At ±0.01 mm spacer tolerance in a 20-knife stack, total width positioning error can reach ±0.2 mm before accounting for knife tolerance.

**Knife clearance** — the horizontal gap between upper and lower knife faces (expressed as % of material thickness) determines edge quality. Incorrect clearance is the most common source of burr and edge defects.

**Stripper rings** — rubber or polyurethane rings mounted on the arbors prevent strips from wrapping onto the knife. Undersized or hardened stripper rings cause strip damage downstream of the cut.

For tooling selection guidance, see [Slitter Knife Material Comparison](/en/wiki/slitter-knife-material-comparison) and [Slitting Machine Selection Guide](/en/wiki/slitting-machine-selection-guide).

## Automation and Control Systems

Modern slitting lines are PLC-controlled with HMI recipe management. Key automation features:

**Recipe storage** — save and recall complete machine settings (speeds, tensions, clearances) for each material/width combination. Eliminates manual re-entry and setup errors on repeat orders.

**Servo-driven knife positioning** — motorized arbor positioning eliminates manual spacer calculation. Width changes that required 60 minutes manually complete in 10–15 minutes with servo positioning.

**Tension feedback control** — closed-loop strip tension measurement and control. Essential for materials where tension variation causes elongation variation, strip camber, or coil shape defects.

**Production data logging** — length, speed, tension, and alarm data logged per coil. Required for quality traceability in automotive and electronics supply chains.

**Predictive maintenance alerts** — vibration monitoring on arbor bearings, motor current trending, and knife life tracking. Allows planned maintenance instead of reactive downtime.

---

## Service Life and Maintenance

A well-maintained industrial slitting line has a mechanical service life of 15–25 years. The major cost centers over this life:

- **Knife replacement and regrinding** — typically the largest variable consumable cost
- **Arbor bearing replacement** — every 3–7 years depending on operating conditions
- **Control system modernization** — typically required at 10–15 years as replacement parts become unavailable
- **Planned mechanical rebuild** — at 10–12 years, a full arbor, drive, and frame inspection with targeted replacement extends service life by 10+ years

**Q: What does a slitting machine do?**
Unwinds a wide master coil, shear-cuts it longitudinally into 2–40+ narrower strips via rotating knife pairs, and rewinds each strip into individual coils.

**Q: What materials can it process?**
Carbon steel, stainless steel, aluminum, copper, brass, electrical steel, and specialty alloys. Foil lines handle material below 0.1 mm. Some lines process plastic film, non-woven, and paper.

**Q: How accurate is the strip width?**
Standard lines: ±0.2–0.5 mm. Precision lines with servo arbor positioning and ±0.002 mm spacers: ±0.05–0.1 mm.

**Q: How long does a slitting machine last?**
15–25 years mechanically. Control systems need modernization at 10–15 years. A planned rebuild at 10–12 years extends life significantly.
