# Slitter Knives: Complete Technical Guide

A slitter knife is a precision-ground circular blade used in coil slitting lines to cut wide sheet metal, foil, or film into narrower strips. The knife's geometry, material grade, and dimensional accuracy determine edge quality, tool life, and the total economics of a slitting operation.

---

## How Slitter Knives Work

Slitter knives operate in matched upper-and-lower pairs mounted on parallel arbors. The two knives overlap slightly in the horizontal plane (penetration depth) and are separated by a precise horizontal clearance. As the coil passes between the rotating knife pairs, material is sheared — not sawn — by the compressive and tensile stresses at the cut point.

This shear mechanism distinguishes slitting from grinding or sawing. A clean slit requires the knife edges to engage the material at the correct geometry and clearance; deviations as small as 0.01 mm produce measurable changes in burr height and edge fracture zone depth.

---

## Knife Types and Configurations

### Solid Circular Slitting Knives

The standard configuration for metal slitting. A single-piece disc of tool steel or carbide, precision-ground on all surfaces. Used for the full range of metals from soft aluminum to hardened stainless steel.

### Segment Knives

Multi-piece knives where replaceable carbide inserts are clamped into a steel body. Economical for large-diameter knife positions where replacing the full disc would be cost-prohibitive. Common on heavy-gauge lines above 8 mm material thickness.

### Crush-Cut Knives (Scoring Knives)

Upper and lower knives with no overlap — the material is crushed and fractured at the cut point rather than shear-cut. Used for paper, non-woven fabrics, and plastic films where a clean shear edge is not required.

### Side-Relief Knives

Knives with a ground relief angle on the side face, reducing friction between the knife face and the slit strip. Used for materials prone to galling onto the knife face, such as pure aluminum or copper.

## Key Dimensional Parameters

### Outer Diameter (OD)

Slitter knives range from 100 mm to over 400 mm in outer diameter. Larger diameters are used on heavy-gauge lines for greater rigidity and to accommodate more regrind cycles before the knife becomes undersized. Tolerances for precision applications: ±0.002 mm.

### Bore Diameter

The arbor fit bore is ground to match the machine arbor with an H7/h6 or H6/h5 fit. Bore concentricity relative to the cutting edge must be within 0.002 mm to avoid runout that causes uneven cutting forces.

### Width (Thickness)

Knife thickness determines the minimum practical strip width and affects knife stack clearance. Precision tolerances: ±0.002 mm. A knife stack tolerance error of ±0.01 mm in a 20-knife arbor accumulates to ±0.2 mm total positioning error — unacceptable for precision strip work.

### Penetration Depth (Overlap)

The vertical overlap between upper and lower knife cutting faces. Typical values: 0.1–0.5 mm for thin material, 0.5–2.0 mm for heavy gauge. Too little overlap causes incomplete cutting; too much increases burr formation and knife wear.

---

## Horizontal Clearance: The Most Critical Parameter

Horizontal clearance is the gap between the cutting faces of the upper and lower knife pair, measured perpendicular to the cut direction. It is typically expressed as a percentage of material thickness.

| Material | Recommended Clearance |
|----------|----------------------|
| Mild carbon steel (CR/HR) | 8–12% of thickness |
| Galvanized / pre-painted steel | 10–14% |
| 304/316 stainless steel | 10–15% |
| Aluminum alloy | 5–8% |
| Copper and brass | 4–6% |
| Electrical steel (silicon steel) | 6–10% |
| PET/BOPP film | 1–3% |

**Too little clearance** produces a rolled burr on the underside of the strip and accelerates knife wear through compressive edge loading.

**Too much clearance** produces a torn, ragged fracture zone — elevated burr height, excessive rollover, and subsurface work-hardening of the strip edge.

---

## Knife Geometry: Face Angle and Edge Profile

### Square-Edge Knives

Both side faces ground at 90° to the cutting face. Standard for most metal slitting applications. The flat side faces provide a well-defined shear plane and predictable clearance control.

### Beveled-Edge Knives

One or both side faces ground at an angle (typically 2–5°). The bevel reduces contact area between the knife face and the emerging strip, lowering side friction. Used for materials prone to adhesion and for very thin foils where friction-induced strip distortion is a concern.

### Radius-Edge Knives

A controlled radius ground on the cutting corner rather than a sharp arris. Used in applications where edge sharpness would cause material damage at the cut entry point, such as soft copper foil for battery applications.

## Material Grades

Knife material determines wear resistance, toughness, and cost-per-cut. The main grades in order of increasing wear resistance:

| Grade | Hardness | Best Application |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| D2 Tool Steel | 58–62 HRC | Paper, non-woven, light film |
| HSS M2 | 62–64 HRC | Mild steel, soft aluminum, medium volume |
| HSS M42 | 65–67 HRC | Stainless steel, high-alloy steel |
| Tungsten Carbide | 88–92 HRA | Electrical steel, copper foil, high volume |
| Ceramic | 92+ HRA | Battery separator, ultra-precision foil |

## Regrinding and Reconditioning

Slitter knives are designed to be reground. Each regrind cycle removes 0.2–0.5 mm of outer diameter by surface grinding the cutting face, restoring the sharp cutting edge. HSS knives can typically be reground 5–10 times before the diameter reduction affects clearance geometry.

**Critical regrinding requirements:**
- Always regrind upper and lower knives as matched pairs
- Maintain OD tolerance to ±0.002 mm after regrinding
- Check arbor runout after reassembly — any wobble above 0.003 mm requires correction
- Inspect bore condition; any bore wear exceeding 0.005 mm can cause seating errors that appear as runout

Carbide knives require diamond grinding wheels and grinding parameters suited to cemented carbide. Standard HSS grinding setups will crack or chip carbide.

## Identifying Knife Wear and Failure Modes

### Edge Rollover
The cutting edge deforms plastically rather than wearing abrasively. Cause: knife material too soft for the material, or penetration depth excessive. Solution: upgrade to harder grade or reduce penetration.

### Edge Chipping
Small chips missing from the cutting arris. Cause: carbide knife with setup errors (runout, misaligned clearance), or impact loading from weld joints. Solution: verify setup tolerances; consider tougher grade.

### Face Galling
Workpiece material adhered to the knife side face. Cause: insufficient clearance or incompatible material/knife combination. Common with pure aluminum and copper. Solution: reduce penetration depth, apply side-relief geometry, or use coated knife faces.

### Uniform Abrasive Wear
Gradual radius growth on the cutting edge. Normal wear mechanism for correctly specified knives. Monitor by measuring strip burr height and schedule regrinding before burr exceeds specification.

## Quality Specifications

For precision strip applications, knife specifications should include:

- OD tolerance: ±0.002 mm
- Width (thickness) tolerance: ±0.002 mm
- Bore concentricity to OD: ≤ 0.002 mm TIR
- Side face flatness: ≤ 0.003 mm per 100 mm radius
- Edge hardness certificate: actual HRC/HRA value, not grade nominal
- Surface roughness on side faces: Ra ≤ 0.4 μm

Generic import knives typically hold ±0.01–0.05 mm on dimensional tolerances — 5–25× looser than precision standards. This tolerance accumulation in a multi-knife arbor stack creates measurable strip width variation and differential wear rates.

**Q: What is a slitter knife?**
A precision circular blade used in pairs to shear-cut wide coils into narrower strips. Not sawn — the material fractures under compressive and tensile stress at the controlled gap between upper and lower knife faces.

**Q: What clearance should I use?**
8–12% of thickness for mild carbon steel; 10–15% for stainless; 5–8% for aluminum; 4–6% for copper. Wrong clearance is the most common cause of poor slit edge quality.

**Q: How often should I regrind?**
When strip burr height exceeds 10% of material thickness, or when edge rollover is visible. Always regrind upper and lower knives as matched pairs.

**Q: HSS vs carbide — which should I use?**
HSS for most applications; carbide for high-volume or abrasive materials (stainless, electrical steel, copper foil). Carbide costs 4–6× more per knife but delivers lower total cost above ~100,000 m/month on difficult materials.
